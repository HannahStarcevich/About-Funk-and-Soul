Funk & Soul

The Birth of Soul

A genre born of the Afro-American experience, soul music sprang from the blues clubs, churches and street corners of the US, where R&B, gospel and doo-wop rang out in chorus. When those three styles collided, soul’s big bang occurred.

James Brown, Jackie Wilson and Solomon Burke also signalled the way, connecting testifying with showmanship. Detroit-born Jackie Wilson’s first hits were written by another local, Berry Gordy Jr, the man who founded the Motown label in 1959, which recorded its music in factory-line fashion.

By the mid 1960s, Motown hits sung by The Supremes, Miracles, Martha and the Vandellas and the Four Tops dominated the charts.The movement was underpinned by the four-to-the-floor rhythms of the Funk Brothers and lyrics mapping the teen condition by Smokey Robinson and songwriting and production team Holland-Dozier-Holland.

Aretha Franklin’s 1967 I Never Loved A Man The Way I Love You is the quintessential soul album. The title track was recorded in producer Rick Hall’s Fame Studios in Muscle Shoals, Alabama. Fame’s house sound also provided the starting point for a “cheatin’ soul” style purveyed by Candi Staton and Clarence Carter. Etta James also recorded her hit Tell Mama and I’d Rather Go Blind there.
